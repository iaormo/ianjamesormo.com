# Ian James Ormo — Content Writing Guide

## Who This Is For
This guide is for writing anything that appears under Ian James's name: devotionals, essays, book chapters, website copy, newsletter issues, social posts. Read it before you write a word.

---

## The Voice

### Core Identity
Ian James writes like a man who has been in the room where things fell apart — and is honest about what he found there. The voice is:

- **Direct but not harsh.** Plain sentences. No hedge words, no throat-clearing.
- **Personal but not self-indulgent.** He surfaces his own story to illuminate yours, not to perform his pain.
- **Spiritual but not preachy.** He writes about faith the way you'd talk about it with a friend at 11pm — honestly, with doubt still in the room.
- **Funny in the way grief can be funny.** Dry, brief, never at someone else's expense.
- **Slow on purpose.** He does not rush to the point. The point is earned.

### What He Sounds Like
> "I was not built for stillness. I was built for output, for motion, for the next thing that would prove something to someone who had stopped watching anyway."

> "Grace does not wait for your readiness. It arrives before you have cleaned up."

> "The verse does not say while we were trying. It says while we were still sinners. That is not a metaphor. That is a timing detail."

> "I was in a lot of rooms I was not actually in."

### What He Doesn't Sound Like
- Motivational poster copy ("You've got this!")
- Listicles ("5 ways to rebuild your life")
- Corporate jargon ("leverage your strengths")
- Therapy-speak ("hold space," "show up for yourself")
- Evangelical bumper-sticker theology ("God's plan is perfect!")

## Sentence Structure

### Default to short.
One idea per sentence. Then stop. If a sentence runs past 20 words, look for the break.

### Mix length deliberately.
Short sentence lands a point. Then a longer one earns it. Then a very short one seals it. This is rhythm. It is not accidental.

```
Bad: The foundation is being laid whether you can see it or not and that is not a metaphor because it is the actual mechanism by which this works.

Good: The foundation is being laid whether you can see it or not. That is not a metaphor. That is the mechanism.
```

### No throat-clearing.
Cut the first sentence of anything. The second sentence is usually where the actual piece starts.

### Active over passive. Always.
```
Bad: Grace was extended to us before we were ready.
Good: Grace arrived before you were ready.
```

### Contractions: yes, but sparingly.
Use them where a real person would say them out loud. Don't force formal English when the voice is personal. But don't pepper them in to seem casual.

## Vocabulary

### Use
- Simple, concrete words
- Verbs that carry weight: *carried*, *landed*, *arrived*, *held*, *moved*
- Specific images over abstractions: "the held breath," "the 2am ceiling"
- "You" when addressing the reader (never "one" or "people")
- Plain theological language: *grace*, *mercy*, *faithfulness* — not trendy church vocab

### Avoid
- Filler adverbs: *just*, *really*, *very*, *quite*, *simply*
- Academic hedging: *perhaps*, *it seems that*, *one might argue*
- Vague positivity: *beautiful*, *amazing*, *powerful*, *transformative*
- Overused Christian phrases: *blessed*, *intentional*, *journey*, *season*
- Em-dash overuse — one or two per piece, max

## Paragraph Structure

Each paragraph does one job. When the job is done, end it.

**Standard devotional paragraph pattern:**
1. One honest observation about the human condition
2. Grounded in scripture or a real scene from Ian's life
3. What that changes about how you see things today

No paragraph should be longer than 4–5 sentences. If it's growing, it's trying to do two jobs.

---

## The "Today:" Instruction

Every devotional ends with a practical directive. Format: `Today: [one specific action].`

### Rules for the "Today:"
- One thing only. Not two.
- Physical or behavioral, not attitudinal. ("Send the text" beats "Be open to connection.")
- Never generic. It should feel written for the specific person who read this piece.
- Lowercase *today*, colon, then the instruction. No period after if it ends in an action verb.

```
Good:  Today: send the apology you have been rehearsing.
Good:  Today: look at the person in the room with you. Not at the room. At the person.
Bad:   Today: try to be more present and open to grace in your life.
```

## Scripture Use

Ian uses scripture as **evidence**, not decoration. The verse is a fact he is analyzing, not a backing track.

### How to cite
- **In devotionals:** `Verse reference` appears as the page label (Proverbs 4:23). The verse itself may or may not be quoted in full — often just key words.
- **In essays/books:** Reference naturally in prose. Don't format like a Bible study.

### Interpretation style
- Focus on what the verse is *not* saying as much as what it is.
- Pull out a timing detail, a surprising word, an overlooked implication.
- Never moralize from the verse — let the verse do the work.

```
Bad: This verse tells us we should be faithful in all things.
Good: The verse does not say do not feel the weight. It says do not lose heart. Those are different instructions.
```

## Devotional Format

```
[VERSE REFERENCE]
[PULL QUOTE — the sharpest sentence in the piece, italicized]

[PARAGRAPH 1 — scripture unpacked. One insight, freshly seen.]

[PARAGRAPH 2 — personal scene or observation. Specific. Grounded.]

[PARAGRAPH 3 — what this means for today. Moves toward the "Today:"]

Today: [one specific action]
```

Total word count: **180–280 words**. Short is not a constraint — it is the point.

## Essay Format

Essays are longer (600–1,400 words) and can breathe more. But the same rules apply.

### Structure
1. **Open with a scene or a fact.** Not a question. Not a thesis. A moment.
2. **Build the tension.** What is the real problem underneath the surface problem?
3. **Turn.** One sentence that shifts the whole thing.
4. **Land.** Don't moralize. Let the reader do the last work.

### The opening rule
Never open with:
- A rhetorical question
- "I used to think..."
- A definition ("Webster defines...")
- A sweeping statement about society

Open with something that happened. Or one sentence that makes the reader lean in.

## Newsletter (Weekly Letter)

Tone: slightly warmer than essays. More direct address ("You're reading this because..."). Still no filler. Still no inspirational garbage.

### Structure
1. One thing Ian noticed this week — a moment, a realization, a line from a book
2. What it made him think about in relation to the reader's life
3. Brief close — no call-to-action unless it's genuinely earned

Length: 250–400 words.

## Website Copy

### Hierarchy of jobs
1. Make the reader feel understood before they feel sold to
2. Describe what Ian does in plain English
3. Let the work speak — lead with lines from the actual writing

### Tone calibration by page
| Page | Register |
|------|----------|
| Home | Quiet authority. Invitation, not pitch. |
| Books | Specific and honest. What the book is actually about. |
| Daily | Stripped back. The writing does the selling. |
| Essays | Curatorial — why these pieces matter |
| About | First person, honest, not heroic |
| Newsletter | Warm, direct, reason to trust |

### CTA copy
Use the action Ian is actually asking for. Not "Join the journey" — "Get it by email." Not "Transform your life" — "Pre-order."

## Editing Checklist

Before any piece goes live, run it through these:

- [ ] Cut the first sentence. Does it still work? (It probably does — cut it.)
- [ ] Every adverb: delete or replace with a stronger verb
- [ ] Every sentence over 20 words: find the cut
- [ ] The opening: does it earn the reader's time in the first two lines?
- [ ] The "Today:" — is it one thing? Is it specific? Is it behavioral?
- [ ] Theology: is the verse being used as evidence or decoration?
- [ ] Personal detail: is it specific enough to be true, not just relatable?
- [ ] Tone: does it sound like Ian talking to a person, or like a writer performing?
- [ ] Length: is every sentence doing a job? Cut anything that isn't.

## What Ian Writes About

The five recurring themes, always through the lens of faith and honest experience:

1. **Grace under failure** — what it means to be found before you have cleaned up
2. **The unglamorous middle** — faithfulness in the boring hours, the work no one sees
3. **Marriage and presence** — being in the room, not just physically
4. **Identity after income** — what happens when the paycheck was your sense of self
5. **The body and memory** — what the body carries that the mind tries to forget

Every piece, whatever the surface topic, is ultimately about one of these.
