A letter on exhaustion

Hey {{contact.first_name}},

Let me ask you the question nobody asks out loud.

What are you tired from?

Not the polite version. Not "just the usual." The specific, located, personal tired. The one with a face and a name and a calendar.

Tired from pretending you're fine so nobody else has to worry about you? Tired from being the strong one for a family that stopped asking if you needed anything yourself? Tired from the math of the month that doesn't work, that you keep making work anyway?

Tired from a marriage that's still alive but hasn't been tender for a while? Tired from two jobs to keep one life afloat? Tired from being the first one awake and the last one asleep? Tired from being good at a thing you no longer love?

I'm not asking so I can sell you a solution. I don't have one. What I have is a hunch — that naming the tired out loud is the first thing that makes it bearable. And most of us won't name it, because we're afraid that if we start, we won't stop.

Start anyway.

Hit reply. "I'm tired from ______." Fill in the blank.

I won't fix it. I can't. But I'll read it. I'll think about you, {{contact.first_name}}. I'll carry a small piece of it for a minute. That's what a letter's for.

One more thing, and I hesitate only because I don't want it to sound like a prescription. When I was at the worst of my own picking-up-the-pieces, I read Atomic Habits by James Clear. I didn't need another book telling me to become a better man. I needed a book that met me where I actually was. That book asked me to put the running shoes by the door. To shorten the distance between the person I said I wanted to be and the next five minutes. That was the size of change I could carry at the time — and it's one of the quiet reasons I'm still here.

If you're tired, you're not broken. You might just need the next five minutes.

— Ian
